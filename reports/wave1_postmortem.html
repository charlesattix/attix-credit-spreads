Phase 7 Wave 1 Post-Mortem

Honest analysis of three failed strategies • What we learned • What's salvageable • What comes next • 2026-04-06
Wave 1 scorecard: 5 strategies tested (EXP-1700, 1710, 1720, 1730, 1660-VRP). 3 failed outright (1700, 1720, 1730). 1 was narrow-but-real (1660 XLF VRP at 3% threshold). 1 is still in progress (1710 0DTE).

This is the honest outcome. Most trading ideas don't work when subjected to real data with walk-forward validation. Rule Zero protects us from learning the wrong lesson ("our backtest shows Sharpe 3.0!") by forcing us to confront the market as it actually is. The failures below are more educational than the wins.

Approach

Short VXX (iPath VIX Short-Term Futures ETN) when the VIX term structure is in contango (VIX/VIX3M < 0.95). The thesis: VXX decays ~5-10%/month in contango due to negative roll yield; shorting it is the textbook VRP harvest. Flat position when VIX > VIX3M (backwardation) to avoid crisis. Inverse-vol position sizing targeting 10% annualized vol.

Why it failed

  1. VXX is a path-dependent ETN, not a futures contract. The VXX roll decay is real but the ETN's price discovery gets polluted by its own rebalancing flows, creator/redeemer arbitrage, and the front two VIX futures rolling daily. It's a noisy 2nd-derivative of the actual VIX futures term structure. We were proxying a proxy.
  2. 4.4% full CAGR with 19.1% max DD is the worst possible return/risk profile. The Sharpe of 0.03 means the strategy essentially moves with random noise — every dollar of return comes with $6 of realized risk. The VRP is real, but VXX captures such a small fraction of it after fees, tracking error, and path dependency that it's indistinguishable from noise over 8 years.
  3. Contango is not a tradable edge alone. VIX/VIX3M < 0.95 held true 70%+ of days from 2018-2025, meaning the strategy was almost always short VXX. When VXX ran up in March 2020, Feb 2022, Aug 2024, etc., the losses wiped out months of grinding contango collection.
  4. Walk-forward OOS Sharpe of 0.27 isn't much better than full-period 0.03. This confirms the failure isn't an overfit issue — the signal itself is marginal on real VXX data.

Root cause

Data proxy mismatch. The VIX roll yield is real in CBOE VIX futures (/VX). VXX is a derivative product of VX futures that adds its own layers of tracking error and negative convexity. Testing the VRP thesis on VXX is like testing crude oil on USO — you get pollution, not signal.

Approach

Engle-Granger cointegration on 45 unique pairs from 10 SPDR sector ETFs (XLF, XLK, XLE, XLU, XLI, XLV, XLP, XLY, XLC, XLRE). Train 2015-2020, select top 5-10 pairs by ADF t-statistic, trade the OLS residual at |z| > 2.0 entry, |z| < 0.5 exit, |z| > 3.5 stop. Walk-forward out-of-sample.

Why it failed

  1. Sector ETFs drifted apart structurally after 2020. XLK (Technology) pulled away from every other sector during COVID and stayed there. XLE bounced 2020 → 2022. XLRE was obliterated by rates 2022-2024. Pairs that looked cointegrated in 2015-2020 broke during the test period — this is a classic regime change failure of cointegration-based trading.
  2. A 4.9% realized vol with 10.3% max DD means the worst drawdowns were 2+ standard deviations. Mean reversion worked on average but left-tail events (sector regime breaks) wiped out cumulative profits. This is exactly the shape of failed statistical arbitrage.
  3. Only 8 pairs passed ADF at 5%. When regime change hits, diversification across 8 loosely-related pairs doesn't help because they all break in the same macro direction. Real statistical arbitrage desks trade hundreds of pairs to dilute regime risk — our 8 isn't enough.
  4. Entry |z| > 2.0 is too permissive for sectors. At |z| 2, you're betting on mean-reversion of a 60-day residual that could easily continue for another 60 days during a trend. The stop at |z| > 3.5 catches the worst trades but by then you've already taken large losses.

Root cause

Cointegration is a 2015-2019 phenomenon. The 2020-2025 period had too many macro-driven sector regime shifts (COVID, rate hikes, AI boom, banking crisis) for pair residuals to behave stationary. The strategy was well-designed but trading in the wrong macro environment.

Approach

TLT/SHY spread z-score mean reversion. 252-day rolling log ratio, enter at |z| > 1.5, exit at |z| < 0.5, stop at |z| > 3.0. Train 2010-2019, test 2020-present on real Yahoo daily bars.

Why it failed as a return source

  1. Nearly zero CAGR (-0.03% over 13.9 years). 38 trades with 50% win rate means this is coin-flip territory. The curve z-score doesn't mean-revert cleanly — long-duration bonds price in policy expectations that can shift without "reverting" for years.
  2. Signal is too slow. 252-day lookback means the strategy enters trades very rarely (38 trades in 14 years) and holds them for weeks-to-months. At such low frequency, the statistical power to detect an edge is tiny even if one exists.
  3. Entry/exit thresholds were fit to pre-2020 curve behavior. The 2022-2024 rate-hiking cycle pushed TLT/SHY far outside its 2010-2019 distribution. |z| crossed 2+ and stayed there — no reversion to the 2010s mean.

The salvage opportunity

The -0.17 SPY correlation and -0.24 EXP-1710 correlation are REAL and non-trivial. A strategy that loses 0.03%/yr but is genuinely negatively correlated with the equity book can still add value as a pure hedge overlay if its drawdowns are small. EXP-1730 max DD is 0.65% — less than 1% of capital — which is better than any hedge we currently pay for.

Salvage proposal

  • Keep it as a 1-3% of portfolio overlay, not a return source. At 0% CAGR and 0.65% DD, it's a cheap diversifier that pays no positive return but doesn't cost much either.
  • Test whether it's ACTUALLY negatively correlated in drawdowns — not just over the full period. If SPY dropped 20% and EXP-1730 was flat, it's a dead-weight hedge. If SPY dropped 20% and EXP-1730 was +2%, it's a cheap insurance premium.
  • Don't lever it. The signal is too marginal. Leveraging a 0% CAGR strategy just amplifies noise.

2. Would EXP-1700 Work with Real VIX Futures?

Short answer: Probably yes, but we'd need CBOE futures data we don't currently have.

The VRP via VIX futures roll is one of the most documented edges in quant literature (Whaley 2013, Moran 2014, Donninger 2019). The edge exists, but VXX does not capture it cleanly. Here's what we'd need to test it properly:

What we'd test differently

  1. Short front-month, long second-month. Calendar spread = pure roll-yield capture with no directional VIX exposure. This is what institutional desks actually do.
  2. Roll on schedule, not on signal. Roll F1→F2 at expiry, never hold into settlement. Path-dependency vanishes.
  3. Size by contango steepness. F2/F1 ratio 1.00-1.05 = half size. F2/F1 > 1.05 = full size. F2/F1 < 1.00 (backwardation) = flat. The signal is the slope, not the level.
  4. Overlay crash insurance. Buy 1-month OTM VIX calls monthly at 10-20% of the short-calendar P&L. Protects against 2020-style events that kill naked VRP shorts.

Recommendation

Kill VXX-based VRP (EXP-1700) permanently. If we want to harvest the VRP beyond what EXP-1660 XLF IV-RV gap already captures, we need to acquire CBOE /VX futures data and rebuild from scratch as EXP-1700-v2 — calendar spread, not ETN short. This would be a Phase 8 data-acquisition project, not a code problem.

3. Honest Lessons Learned

  1. Proxy instruments lie. Testing VRP via VXX, credit spreads via XLF put spreads, or rates via SHV instead of the actual security-of-interest adds 50-100 basis points of tracking noise per month. Rule Zero should extend to instrument selection: use the real thing or don't test it.
  2. Cointegration is unstable across regime changes. Any strategy that assumes a stable relationship between two assets needs a regime-detection overlay, AND needs to be retrained when the regime shifts. Static pair selection trained on 2015-2020 and deployed in 2020-2025 is backtest-fit not market-adapted.
  3. Negative correlation at zero return is not worthless. EXP-1730's -0.17 SPY correlation at a tiny max DD (0.65%) is cheaper than buying SPY puts. As a portfolio construction component, low-return low-correlation strategies can improve risk-adjusted returns of the combined book even if they standalone look terrible.
  4. Walk-forward OOS Sharpe is the only metric that matters. Full-period Sharpe on EXP-1700 was 0.03. OOS Sharpe was 0.27 — better, but still not worth deploying. We now know the floor is OOS Sharpe > 0.50 for any new strategy; below that, kill immediately.
  5. Small trade counts are a fatal data problem. EXP-1730 had 38 trades in 14 years. Our Sharpe estimate has such wide confidence intervals that a -0.29 Sharpe is indistinguishable from a +0.29 Sharpe. Strategies generating < 100 trades over the test period should require much stronger priors to be worth pursuing.
  6. The hardest failures are the ones with a real thesis. EXP-1700's VRP thesis is correct. EXP-1720's cointegration-mean-reversion thesis is correct. EXP-1730's curve-trading thesis is correct. The strategies failed on implementation details, not the core ideas. This is important — it tells us what to fix, not what to abandon.

4. Three New Strategy Ideas to Fill the Sharpe Gap

After three failures and one narrow success (EXP-1660 XLF), we need fundamentally different strategy types, not more variations on what failed. Thinking outside the box:

Idea A: EXP-1750 — Overnight Drift in SPY

Thesis: The "overnight effect" in US equities is one of the most documented anomalies in finance (Cooper 2008, Kelly & Clark 2011). SPY generates ~70-80% of its lifetime return during the overnight session (4pm close to 9:30am open next day) and is roughly flat during regular trading hours. The edge has been stable for 30+ years and survives transaction costs.

Implementation

  • Entry: Buy SPY shares at 3:58pm ET close, sell at 9:35am open.
  • Capital: 100% allocation every night.
  • Skip days: Known event nights (FOMC, NFP, CPI) per the Thursday/Friday calendar.
  • Overlay: Short 25-delta SPY weekly calls during the day session to earn day-time theta and offset overnight beta exposure.

Data we have

READY NOW — SPY daily OHLC (open + close) from Yahoo is already in our data pipeline. The underlying strategy doesn't need options at all for the core test. The overlay test would use IronVault SPY weekly options we already have.

Expected profile

  • Target Sharpe: 0.8-1.2 (long-term academic finding)
  • Correlation to EXP-1220: ~0.4 (both long-ish equity risk)
  • Max DD: 12-18% (overnight tail events)
  • Capacity: $10B+ (SPY daily volume)

Why it could work where VXX failed

This trades the actual security (SPY), uses real trading sessions (no roll), and has 30+ years of academic validation. The "overnight drift" is a behavioral anomaly (market makers hedging) that persists despite being well-known because the arbitrage is constrained by overnight capital costs.

Idea B: EXP-1760 — Earnings Announcement IV Crush

Thesis: Implied volatility on single-name stocks systematically overstates realized vol in the 1-2 days around earnings announcements. Selling short-dated straddles on well-capitalized large caps at the close before earnings and buying back at the open after captures the IV crush while being neutral to direction. Academic evidence: Gao & Xing (2020) show the "earnings IV premium" averages 50-100bps per announcement on liquid stocks.

Implementation

  • Universe: S&P 100 stocks with liquid weekly options. Need at least 10K daily volume on ATM strikes.
  • Entry: 3:58pm ET the day before earnings — sell ATM straddle in the weekly expiring 3-5 days later.
  • Exit: 9:35am ET the day after earnings — buy back the straddle.
  • Position size: 5% of capital per trade, max 10 concurrent positions.
  • Filter: Skip if IV rank < 70% (cheap options = no edge) or market cap < $10B (too illiquid).

Data we need

PARTIAL — We don't have single-name options in IronVault (only SPY + sector ETFs). To test this we'd need:

  • Earnings calendar (free from Yahoo/Finnhub/IEX Cloud)
  • Single-name option quotes on AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, META, NVDA, AMZN, JPM, BAC, WMT, HD, etc. (Polygon options tier: $29/mo for delayed, $199/mo for real-time)
  • Historical IV rank data (Polygon or IVolatility)

Expected profile

  • Target Sharpe: 1.5-2.5 (earnings vol crush is stronger than index VRP)
  • Correlation to EXP-1220: ~0.0 (single-name idiosyncratic)
  • Max DD: 10-15% (one big earnings miss can blow up)
  • Trade count: 40 earnings/quarter × 4 quarters = 160 trades/year — plenty of statistical power

Why this beats our failures

The single-name earnings IV crush is a different kind of edge than index-level VRP. It comes from retail option demand for earnings lottery tickets, not from market-maker hedging flows. Because retail flow is sticky, the premium persists. Unlike EXP-1700's VXX proxy problem, here we'd trade the actual underlyings where the anomaly lives.

Idea C: EXP-1770 — Dispersion Trading (Index vs Single-Names)

Thesis: Index options (SPX, SPY) systematically price in more correlation than is realized, because institutional portfolio insurance creates structural demand for index puts that isn't matched by individual stock put demand. This means implied correlation (derived from index IV vs average component IV) is persistently higher than realized correlation. Selling index straddles while buying individual-name straddles captures the correlation premium.

Implementation

  • Short leg: Sell 1-month SPY ATM straddle.
  • Long leg: Buy 1-month ATM straddles on 10-20 largest S&P components (AAPL, MSFT, NVDA, GOOGL, META, AMZN, BRK, LLY, JPM, V, UNH, XOM, JNJ, WMT, MA, PG, HD, CVX, BAC, KO). Weight by index weight.
  • Delta-hedge: Daily, to isolate vol/correlation exposure.
  • Hold: 5-15 days, rebalance at half the position every week.

Data we need

PARTIAL — Same data gap as EXP-1760 (single-name options). Plus we'd need index component weights (free from S&P or etf.com). The strategy is complex to run but the data requirements overlap with the earnings play, so one subscription unlocks both.

Expected profile

  • Target Sharpe: 1.2-1.8 (dispersion is a well-known edge, typical institutional Sharpe 1.5)
  • Correlation to EXP-1220: ~-0.2 (short index vol = inverse of crisis hedge)
  • Correlation to EXP-1660: ~0.3 (both short vol in some sense)
  • Max DD: 8-15% (correlation regime shifts during crashes)
  • Capital efficiency: 1 position = 21 contracts × 1-month straddles, so $200K-500K buying power per dispersion cycle

Why this is different from what we've tried

Dispersion captures a risk premium that doesn't exist in single-asset VRP (which we already have via EXP-1660). It exploits the structural difference between index and single-name option pricing. Academic evidence is strong (Driessen, Maenhout, Vilkov 2009), and it's one of the core strategies of major vol arb desks (Capstone, Parallax, Citadel). It's complex but defensible.
